Champion Chase

Hybrid-casual action survival game for Android

CC

Overview

Champion Chase was built as the studio's first serious hybrid-casual title: accessible survival combat layered with upgrades, themed worlds, boss encounters, and a lightweight gems-and-coins economy. It shipped on Google Play with ads and in-app purchases, giving the team a real production release to launch and iterate on.

Problem

The studio needed a title that moved beyond simple arcade execution into a more product-shaped mobile game with progression hooks, content variety, monetization, and room for post-launch updates.

My Role

Team Lead and Senior Unity Developer. I led technical execution in Unity, shaped the gameplay architecture and feature roadmap, implemented UI and core systems, and helped take the game from concept to live release and later updates.

Team

Small indie team setup. I led engineering execution across development and release.

Technical Approach

The game was structured around short action sessions backed by persistent progression. I focused on reusable gameplay and UI systems so new abilities, upgrades, worlds, and balance updates could be added without turning the project into one-off feature code.

Key Systems and Decisions

  • Designed for hybrid-casual progression instead of a flat score-chaser loop.
  • Kept combat abilities extensible so powers and upgrades could scale with content.
  • Structured progression through themed worlds, escalation, and boss encounters.
  • Shipped in a way that supported post-launch fixes and additional level content.

Constraints and Tradeoffs

The project needed to stay accessible and lightweight for mobile players while still feeling like a fuller product with progression, monetization, and enough content variety to justify live iteration after launch.

Outcome and Impact

Champion Chase shipped publicly on Google Play, reached a live store rating of 5.0 from 12 reviews and 50+ downloads on the listing you shared, and received post-launch updates including bug fixes and a new level. The strongest signal here is not scale but proof of shipping, learning, and iteration.

Media and Proof

Public proof now includes production screenshots showing live combat density, the store-facing challenge loop, and the upgrade economy. Together they support the gameplay, progression, and product framing described in this case study.

Future Improvements

Ludo King

Platform adaptation for TV and Windows-style play

LK

Overview

Ludo King is a large-scale live multiplayer board game product. My work focused on extending the experience beyond its portrait mobile roots by helping adapt it for TV and Windows-oriented contexts, including landscape UI conversion, platform-fit interaction changes, and prototype validation around voice chat.

Problem

A phone-first portrait game does not automatically translate well to larger screens. Layout, readability, navigation, and social interaction flows all need rethinking for TV and desktop-style environments while preserving the familiar multiplayer loop users already know.

My Role

Platform-focused Unity Engineer working on porting and UX adaptation. I helped convert core game flows and UI from portrait to landscape, adapted interaction patterns for larger-screen contexts, and validated multiplayer and voice-chat behavior in the adapted experience.

Team

Worked within a larger live-game team across engineering, UI, QA, and release stakeholders.

Technical Approach

The work was about proper platform adaptation rather than simply scaling the original UI. I focused on preserving gameplay consistency while reworking screen use, readability, navigation, and input expectations for TV and Windows-style play.

Key Systems and Decisions

  • Converted the experience to landscape instead of relying on portrait-first layouts.
  • Preserved familiar multiplayer flows while adapting platform UX.
  • Treated input and navigation as platform-specific concerns.
  • Prototype-tested voice chat behavior in the adapted context before overcommitting.

Constraints and Tradeoffs

Because this was a scaled live product, changes had to respect the familiarity of the core experience while making it workable for non-phone environments with different viewing distance, navigation patterns, and platform expectations.

Outcome and Impact

I contributed to platform adaptation on one of the world''s largest casual multiplayer games. The public footprint currently shows 100Cr+ downloads, 1Cr+ reviews, Windows availability, and a dedicated Android TV presence. The strongest portfolio signal here is platform translation work inside a massive live product, not ownership of the core game itself.

Media and Proof

Public proof now includes both landscape TV-style screens and portrait mobile product shots. Together they make the platform-adaptation story easier to evaluate: what the original mobile experience looked like, what larger-screen layout required, and how social features such as voice chat fit into the shipped product context.

Future Improvements

The strongest next upgrade here would be a simple annotated before-and-after comparison showing how navigation, spacing, and readability changed between portrait mobile and landscape TV contexts.

Stack

Bright Words

Live mobile word puzzle game with scalable content operations

BW

Overview

Bright Words combines word search, crossword, and scramble mechanics with positivity-themed content such as affirmations and motivational quotes. From an engineering perspective, the important part was making the game scalable both as gameplay and as a content product, with large level progression, remotely updateable content, and lightweight multiplayer support.

Problem

The product needed to do more than ship a fixed set of puzzle levels. It had to support a large and extensible level catalog, ongoing tuning, and updates without depending on a full client release for every change.

My Role

Team Lead and Senior Unity Developer. I led technical execution in Unity across gameplay systems, UI, scalable level and content handling, live-ops-friendly configuration flows, and lightweight multiplayer support.

Team

Built within a small indie team where I covered broad technical scope across gameplay, architecture, and release support.

Technical Approach

I treated Bright Words as a content-driven product rather than a hardcoded puzzle app. Levels and content were designed to be maintainable at scale, with JSON-driven data and Remote Config support so selected tuning and content behavior could evolve without constant rebuilds.

Key Systems and Decisions

  • Separated level and content data from hardcoded client logic.
  • Used Firebase Remote Config for live tuning and operational flexibility.
  • Kept multiplayer lightweight and practical for the product scope.
  • Structured the game to support a large published level catalog more efficiently.

Constraints and Tradeoffs

The challenge was balancing broad casual accessibility with enough operational flexibility to support ongoing content updates, daily-challenge style engagement, and a large level pipeline without making the app brittle to maintain.

Outcome and Impact

Bright Words shipped publicly, reached 10K+ downloads on Google Play, maintained a 4.8-star rating across 356 reviews, and publicly promoted a catalog of 1000+ levels. The stronger portfolio signal is that it demonstrates scalable content architecture and live product thinking, not just puzzle implementation.

Media and Proof

Public proof now includes production screenshots showing puzzle flow, content-driven presentation, and the game's motivational UI layer. Together they reinforce the case study's claims about scalable content handling and a polished live mobile product.

Future Improvements

The next upgrade for this case study is a short gameplay clip or architecture note that shows how JSON-driven content and Remote Config supported ongoing updates without frequent client rebuilds.

Stack
